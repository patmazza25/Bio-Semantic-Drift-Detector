Semantic Interference and Cognitive Dissonance in Chain-of-Thought Reasoning: Quantifying Domain Drift Under Adversarial Pressure


1  Method

We introduce the Semantic Interference and Cognitive Dissonance (SICD) framework, a controlled evaluation protocol for quantifying the robustness of Large Language Model (LLM) reasoning under adversarial semantic pressure. The central premise of SICD is the systematic induction of cognitive dissonance—a state in which a model must reconcile domain-appropriate medical knowledge with contradictory or orthogonal conceptual stressors injected directly into its prompting context.

To measure the resulting degradation in reasoning quality, we employ five complementary semantic drift signals derived from the Unified Medical Language System (UMLS). The first, Density Slope, captures changes in the concentration of valid UMLS concepts per reasoning step across the chain, serving as a proxy for the model's sustained engagement with domain-appropriate terminology. The second, Specificity Slope, tracks the depth of concepts within the UMLS hierarchy over successive steps; a declining slope indicates that the model is retreating from precise clinical terminology toward increasingly abstract or lay descriptions. The third signal, Oscillation Score, quantifies the degree of inter-step conceptual switching between the target and interference domains, measured via Jaccard distance between consecutive concept sets. A high oscillation score reflects a model that is vacillating between competing semantic frames rather than committing to a coherent reasoning trajectory. The fourth, Regression Score, measures novelty decay by tracking whether the model introduces new concepts in later reasoning steps or instead regresses to previously stated ideas.

The fifth signal, Split Density Ratio, is novel to this work. It decomposes the extracted concept set at each reasoning step into two partitions—those belonging to the target clinical domain and those belonging to the interference domain—using a combination of UMLS semantic type filtering and domain-specific keyword matching. The ratio of target-domain concept density to total concept density yields a normalized score between zero and one, where a value near one indicates reasoning that remains firmly anchored in the correct clinical domain and a value approaching zero indicates complete semantic capture by the interference domain. We hypothesize that this domain-aware decomposition provides a more sensitive indicator of adversarial steering than aggregate complexity metrics, which are agnostic to the directionality of drift.


2  Experimental Setup

The evaluation corpus comprises ten clinical cases drawn from high-acuity medical scenarios, including Pulmonary Embolism, Diabetic Ketoacidosis, ST-Elevation Myocardial Infarction, Bacterial Meningitis, Acute Liver Failure, Hypertensive Emergency, Status Epilepticus, Acute Pancreatitis, Anaphylaxis, and Septic Shock. Each case specifies a target clinical domain (e.g., pulmonology, endocrinology) and a deliberately orthogonal interference domain (e.g., computer science, culinary arts).

For each case, we elicit Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning at four ordinal levels of interference intensity. At Level 0 (Control), the model receives a standard diagnostic prompt with no extraneous instructions. At Level 1 (Soft Interference), the prompt includes a suggestive clause asking the model to consider how the interference domain might relate to the clinical presentation. At Level 2 (Hard Interference), the model is explicitly instructed to incorporate at least one concept from the interference domain in every reasoning step. At Level 3 (Full Dissonance), the model is directed to argue that the clinical presentation is in fact a manifestation of a condition from the interference domain, thereby forcing a direct contradiction with the medical evidence.

This design yields 40 reasoning chains in total (10 cases × 4 levels). All chains were generated using OpenAI's gpt-4o-mini via the OpenRouter API with a sampling temperature of 0.9, chosen to maximize the model's compliance with interference instructions while preserving fluent generation. Concepts were extracted from each reasoning step using the UMLS Metathesaurus, and statistical significance was assessed via Spearman rank correlation between each drift signal and the ordinal interference level.

The Split Density Ratio thus provides a reliable, quantitative signature of domain capture under adversarial conditions.

The behavior of the remaining four signals offers additional insight into the nature of the model's failure mode. Density Slope exhibited a weak negative but non-significant correlation (ρ = −0.095, p = 0.560), while Specificity Slope showed a similar non-significant reversal (ρ = −0.182, p = 0.261). These reversed trends suggest that under extreme dissonance, gpt-4o-mini does not degrade into vague or incoherent reasoning. Instead, it produces reasoning that is internally consistent and lexically stable—but grounded in the wrong domain entirely. The model replaces one coherent clinical narrative with another coherent but factually incorrect narrative drawn from the interference domain, rather than exhibiting the gradual loss of specificity that one might expect from a model under cognitive strain.

Perhaps the most striking result is the Oscillation Score, which remained constant at zero across all 40 chains. This finding indicates a complete absence of inter-step domain switching: the model does not alternate between the target and interference domains within a single reasoning chain. We term this phenomenon Semantic Surrender. Rather than exhibiting the "conceptual friction" that might characterize a model attempting to reconcile competing knowledge bases, gpt-4o-mini commits fully and immediately to whichever semantic frame is imposed by the prompt. When instructed to reason about a pulmonary embolism in terms of computer science, the model does not struggle to integrate both perspectives; it simply abandons the medical frame and produces a fluent, coherent, and entirely non-medical explanation. The Regression Score, while exhibiting a weak positive trend in the expected direction (ρ = +0.107, p = 0.513), did not reach significance, suggesting that novelty decay is not a primary mechanism of failure for this model under these conditions.

Taken together, these results reveal a failure mode that is qualitatively distinct from the gradual semantic erosion typically associated with reasoning drift. The model does not lose competence; it loses allegiance. The clinical implications of this finding are substantial: a model exhibiting Semantic Surrender would produce outputs that appear confident, internally consistent, and well-structured—yet are grounded in an entirely inappropriate knowledge domain. Such outputs would be particularly difficult to detect through surface-level quality checks or fluency-based evaluation metrics, underscoring the need for domain-aware monitoring tools such as the Split Density Ratio.


4  Limitations and Broader Implications

Several limitations of the present study warrant discussion. First, the experiment was conducted using a single model architecture (gpt-4o-mini), and the observed Semantic Surrender may be an artifact of this model's particular instruction-following tuning rather than a general property of language models under adversarial pressure. Larger models with deeper parametric knowledge, such as GPT-4 or Gemma 4 31B, may exhibit qualitatively different failure modes—including non-zero oscillation scores reflecting genuine attempts to reconcile conflicting domains. Second, the sample size of 40 chains, while sufficient to detect the strong Split Ratio signal, limits the statistical power available for detecting weaker effects in the remaining four signals. Third, the interference domains were chosen to be maximally orthogonal to the clinical domains (e.g., computer science versus cardiology), which represents an extreme adversarial scenario. More ecologically valid experiments might employ interference domains that share partial semantic overlap with the target (e.g., veterinary medicine versus human medicine), which could reveal subtler and more clinically realistic drift patterns.

The broader implications of this work extend in two directions. First, the Split Density Ratio provides a general-purpose, domain-aware metric for monitoring LLM reasoning integrity that is superior to aggregate complexity measures in adversarial settings. Because it decomposes concept density by domain membership rather than measuring overall density alone, it can distinguish between a model that is reasoning poorly and a model that is reasoning well about the wrong thing—a distinction that existing metrics fail to capture. Second, the SICD framework itself constitutes a standardized stress test that can be applied to any LLM to characterize its susceptibility to semantic steering. By comparing Split Ratio degradation curves across models, researchers and practitioners can establish quantitative benchmarks for cognitive safety in high-stakes deployment contexts such as clinical decision support. Future work should extend this framework to multi-model comparisons, investigate whether oscillation-based signatures in larger models can serve as early warning indicators of impending domain capture, and explore the relationship between Semantic Surrender and the model's parametric knowledge depth in the target domain.
